# Email marketing: the highest-ROI channel nobody runs right

**What this is:** the full playbook for email, the most versatile and highest-ROI tool in marketing. This covers what email is FOR, the three kinds of email, the five campaign types that map to the customer journey, and operator-grade tactics on frequency, offers, subject lines, email structure, and the automations that print money. Reach for this when someone asks how to build a list, how to write emails that sell, how often to send, why their emails aren't converting, or whether email is even worth it anymore. It's also the natural follow-on to a CVO conversation: CVO is the machine, email is the channel that moves people through it.

**Credit where it's due:** the strategic backbone here (the three email types, the five campaign phases) comes from DigitalMarketer's playbook, the Ryan Deiss world the CVO material comes from. The in-the-trenches tactics, the offer wheel, the email-type menu, the automations, come from a book called **"Just Fucking Send It" by Ian Stanley.** Stanley is a 12-year email copywriter with north of $200M in copy-driven sales, and his whole thesis is that the unsent perfect email makes zero dollars. I've run these principles for real money, and what follows is how I actually apply them.

## Why email still wins

Email gets declared dead every couple of years and it keeps producing the highest ROI of any channel. The number people throw around is roughly $42 back for every $1 spent. But the real reason it wins is ownership: your list is an asset you own outright. Social reach is rented from a platform that can change the rules, throttle you, or pull the plug tomorrow. I've said it before and I mean it. Grow the following everywhere, but always be converting it into a list you actually control. That's the one you keep.

And here's the quiet advantage: almost everyone is bad at email. Being merely decent puts you in the top tier.

Email is also the most versatile tool you have. The same channel does branding, engagement, acquisition, retention, direct sales, reactivation, traffic, and referrals. No other channel covers that much ground at that cost.

The money gap is real. The rough industry average is about $1 per subscriber per month. Operators who run it right report $5 to $15 per subscriber per month. That gap isn't magic copy. It's frequency plus the systems below, especially the consumption sequences. Same list, five to fifteen times the money, because of HOW it's worked.

## How I think about email: the bank account

Every email you send is a deposit, a withdrawal, or neutral.

Every time you give people content and don't ask for anything, that's a deposit. Every time you ask for a sale, that's a withdrawal. If everything you ever send is a pitch, you drain the account, and people unsubscribe and start to hate you. So you alternate.

Here's roughly what I do, but test your own list because every niche is different. One week I run an offer: open it Wednesday, close it Sunday, send three to four emails in between. One opener that's mostly content, one that's straight "here's the offer," one that says "this is closing, act now," then a final email a few hours before the deadline sent only to the people who clicked any of the previous emails: "this is literally your last chance." Then the next week, no pitch at all. Three or four really good content emails, pure goodwill, refilling the account.

Deposits, then withdrawals. Keep the balance positive or you churn through your list and burn it into the ground. The subscribers who stick around for years are the bank account working.

## The whole thing in four lines

Stanley compresses his book into four lines, and they're worth memorizing because most email failures are a violation of one of them:

1. Send more emails.
2. Send better emails.
3. Send the RIGHT emails at the RIGHT time.
4. Don't be boring.

And the meta-rule: the unsent "perfect" email makes zero dollars. The good-enough email that actually ships beats it every single time. Perfectionism is the most expensive habit in this channel.

## The 3 types of email

Every email you ever send is one of three types. The average marketer only uses the third one, the sales blast, and that's exactly why their email doesn't work.

### Type 1: Transactional (automated, triggered by an action)

These fire when someone does something: buys, resets a password, returns an item. Here's the part almost nobody acts on: the average revenue per transactional email runs roughly 2x to 5x higher than a standard bulk email. Why? Because the person just took an action and is actively paying attention. Their attention is already on you.

And almost every business sends these as dead, templated system messages. That's a massive missed opportunity. Every transactional email is a chance to nudge the customer one step further.

The eight transactional types and how to actually leverage each:

1. **Order confirmation.** Highest open rate of any email type, period. Add a related product recommendation or a referral ask. The big ecommerce players milk this expertly.
2. **Purchase receipt.** High open rate, rarely leveraged. Add an upsell or a "here's what to do next" nudge.
3. **Shipping notice.** Renews the buyer's excitement. Ask for a social share or a friend referral while they're in it.
4. **Account creation.** They just got access to something new. Ask them to share that excitement.
5. **Return confirmation.** A re-engagement opportunity, not a dead end. Offer a different product or a save-the-sale discount.
6. **Support follow-up.** If support went well, ask for a review or hand them a coupon.
7. **Password reminder.** Usually just a reset link. Add an upcoming offer or a relevant event note.
8. **Unsubscribe confirmation.** Even here, you can offer something that re-engages them on the way out.

The principle: tiny tweaks to system emails compound into real movement, because these are the emails people actually open.

### Type 2: Relational (sent to nurture, not to sell)

These go out regularly to engage your subscribers and deliver value, NOT primarily to sell. Relationship first, and the sale gets easier later.

The eight relational types:

1. **New subscriber welcome.** Sent immediately on signup. Sets expectations and delivers value right away.
2. **Gated content delivery.** Hands over the lead magnet they asked for. They just traded you their email, so the relationship is established (you just had coffee), which makes this the natural moment to also sell them on being a subscriber and, if you have one, to make your tripwire offer. It stays relational and makes the soft pitch at the same time.
3. **Newsletter or blog content.** Distributes your new content to the list.
4. **Webinar or event confirmation.** Confirms the commitment, restates the value.
5. **Survey or review request.** Learn what subscribers care about so you can segment and tailor offers.
6. **Social or product update.** Company and product changes. Build anticipation for what's coming.
7. **Contest announcement.** Your current subscribers should hear it first.
8. **Referral request.** After ANY positive interaction (a purchase, a problem solved, a kind reply), ask.

Relational emails must sound human, not scripted. Always deliver value. Always spell out the next step. Never leave them guessing what to do.

### Type 3: Promotional (sent to generate sales)

The eight promotional types:

1. **Promotional content.** Content that sells while delivering real value. A genuinely useful resource that also points at your offer.
2. **New gated content.** A fresh lead magnet sent to existing subscribers to re-engage and segment them.
3. **Sale announcement.** Gets the most engagement of any email type when the subject line is right.
4. **New product release.** Tell your hyper-buyers, the people who want everything you make.
5. **Webinar announcement.** Your most engaged people will register.
6. **Event announcement.** Same logic, real-world version.
7. **Trial offers.** Lower-friction entry for the hesitant.
8. **Upgrade offers.** Sent to existing customers to move them up the ladder.

The discipline that separates pros from spammers: you don't only send type 3. You earn the right to sell with types 1 and 2, then you sell.

## Broadcast vs autoresponder

Your email tool gives you two delivery modes and you need both:

- **Broadcast.** Sent manually to your whole list or a segment. Best for promotions, content, and anything timely.
- **Autoresponder (triggered).** Built once in advance, fires automatically when a subscriber does a triggering action. Best for nurture, welcome sequences, and post-purchase flows.

Autoresponders are leverage. You write them once and they sell forever. They stabilize cash flow because they run whether you show up that day or not. Broadcasts are the living conversation with your list, the relationship and ongoing monetization. You need both. Automation for the machine, broadcasts for the heartbeat.

One caution: just because you CAN trigger something doesn't mean you SHOULD. Over-automation creates long, mechanical campaigns that trap a subscriber in one phase forever. Automate the repeatable stuff, keep a human hand on the rest.

## The 8 most common automation triggers

Set up automated campaigns off these events:

1. New subscriber: welcome and indoctrination
2. Lead magnet request: automated delivery of the asset
3. Event registration: confirmation plus reminders
4. Purchase: receipt, onboarding, ascension
5. Clicking a link in a segmentation campaign: they raised their hand on a topic, engage them on it
6. Excitement about your brand: referral request after positive engagement
7. Cart or checkout abandonment: reminder to finish (one of the highest-leverage triggers there is)
8. Going quiet: re-engagement campaign for inactive subscribers

## The 5 phases of email marketing

This is the backbone. Five distinct campaign types, each aligned to a stage of the customer journey: stranger, subscriber, buyer, repeat buyer, advocate. Build all five and email stops being a chore and becomes a revenue engine. Most businesses build one or two and plateau.

### Phase 1: The Indoctrination Campaign

**What it is:** a triggered sequence sent immediately after someone subscribes.
**Job:** welcome them, tell them what to expect, tell them what to do next.

Five steps:

1. Welcome them and introduce yourself.
2. Restate in three or four bullets the benefits they get as a subscriber.
3. Tell them what to expect (how often, what kind of content) AND what to do (whitelist you, save your address).
4. Walk them through whitelisting with explicit instructions. This protects your deliverability long term.
5. Send your best stuff: your most-shared, most-loved, highest-engagement content.

Treat indoctrination like a first date. Show up in your best clothes, tell your best stories, focus on the relationship, not the close. You're earning the right to be in their inbox.

### Phase 2: The Engagement Campaign

**What it is:** an interest-based triggered campaign sent after a subscriber does something that signals interest in a topic.
**Job:** turn a subscriber into a buyer by prescribing the next logical step based on what they're interested in RIGHT NOW.

Four steps:

1. Acknowledge the action they just took.
2. Overcome the objections they're feeling. Don't get tactical here. Address the FEELINGS keeping them from acting, not the spec sheet.
3. Prescribe the next logical step. Spell it out plainly.
4. Ask them to buy.

### Phase 3: The Ascension Campaign

**What it is:** an interest-based triggered campaign that makes a relevant offer right after a triggering behavior, usually a purchase.
**Job:** turn a new buyer into a multi-buyer, speed up the journey.

Three steps:

1. Reference what they just did. Congratulate them. Ride the positive energy.
2. Overcome objections to the next step.
3. Spell out that next step.

Before you send any ascension email, ask two questions:

- What is the next step I actually want them to take?
- Do I have any real reason to believe they're ready to take it?

If they're not ready, don't ask. Pushing too fast makes people uncomfortable and they leave. Nurture, then ask. The timing of the ask matters as much as the ask itself.

### Phase 4: The Segmentation Campaign

**What it is:** a manual promotional broadcast sent to your whole list whose goal is to segment people by interest.
**Job:** re-engage people who are stuck by piquing interest, and get them to self-sort by what they care about now.

This is one of the few campaigns that is NOT automated on purpose. It's a deliberate broadcast designed to surface what each subscriber currently wants. When they click on a topic, you now know they care about it, and you can follow up accordingly.

The counterintuitive principle that makes it work: send MORE emails to people who engage with a topic, FEWER to people who don't. It feels backwards, but it works, because it proves you're listening instead of carpet-bombing everyone with everything.

Three ways to run one:

- **Content.** Blog posts, videos, gated assets. A click equals an interest signal.
- **Special offers.** Coupons, flash sales, time-sensitive promos. Action equals topic interest.
- **Events.** Webinars, demos, workshops, calls. A time investment is the strongest signal of all.

### Phase 5: The Re-Engagement Campaign

**What it is:** a triggered campaign for any subscriber who hasn't opened or clicked in about 90 days.
**Job:** call out the inactive, get them re-excited, or cleanly let them go.

How I run it: once someone's gone quiet for around 90 days, I fire a short campaign, 3 emails roughly a week apart, then wait one more week. Across those emails you:

1. Ask directly: "I noticed you haven't opened my emails. Is everything okay?"
2. Remind them what they get as a subscriber.
3. Tell them what they've missed. Send your best content, same as indoctrination.

And the hard rule: if they don't click anything in that whole campaign, they're done. They get a suppressed tag and come off the list. Dead subscribers raise your costs, tank your deliverability, and they're the ones most likely to mark you as spam. Clean the list regularly. A smaller engaged list beats a big rotting one every time.

## The tactics that actually move money

The five phases are the skeleton. Now the muscle. This is where most of the difference between a $1-per-subscriber list and a $5-to-$15 list lives.

### Frequency: send more than you think

This is the single most common place people leave money on the table.

The floor is one email a week. If you're sending monthly, you're not running a list, you're sitting on one. The sweet spot for most businesses is somewhere around three to six times a week, though you need to test your own list and find what it will bear. What I can tell you is the businesses that email most often tend to make the most money.

Consistency beats volume. Three times a week, forever, outperforms a streaky pattern of daily-then-nothing. Pick a cadence you can actually hold and hold it. Reliability trains the list to expect you.

We usually send at 9 in the morning or 2 in the afternoon, and that works best for our list, but experiment and watch your open rates. Some email tools now do predictive sending (ActiveCampaign is one), which sends each person at the time they've historically opened your emails instead of blasting the whole list at once. Worth looking into if you're on a tool that supports it.

One specific move on promo closes: on the last couple of days, increase your send volume, but gate the extra sends to people who have engaged recently. The people who've been clicking get hit harder, the people who've gone quiet don't. You maximize revenue without spiking complaints.

### You're only annoying if you suck

The fear that more emails will annoy people is what holds most operators back, and it's mostly wrong. You're only annoying if you're boring, if you don't provide value, or if you're sending stuff they never signed up for. Send what they expected, make it good, and frequency is a feature, not a nuisance.

Reframe unsubscribes: they're people walking out of a store that wasn't their style. Not a tragedy, not a metric worth obsessing over. The metrics worth watching are opens (keep them healthy, roughly 25 to 35%, for deliverability), clicks, and sales. One caveat on opens: some email clients now auto-load images and inflate open rates, so it's a softer signal than it used to be. Clicks and sales don't lie.

### Be personal (on purpose)

Share your own stories, your screwups, the behind-the-scenes stuff. Connection happens around the rough edges, not the polished version of you. The tell that it's working: subscribers start asking about your life, your dog, your situation, by name. The one rule: always tie the personal story back to a lesson or a point for the reader. Personal in service of value, not just for its own sake.

### The Core Offer Wheel

The blank-page problem ("what do I email about today?") is mostly an offer problem. The Core Offer Wheel gives every email a job. Four spokes, rotate through them:

1. **Low-Ticket Front End (under roughly $100).** The buyer generator. A cheap, genuinely great entry product that converts cold-ish traffic into buyers. The secret to turning a $5 buyer into a $5,000 buyer later is to make the $5 thing actually great. That's what earns the trust to ascend them. And remember: the hardest thing in business is getting a stranger to pull out their card the first time. Once they've done that, everything gets easier.
2. **High-Ticket Back End ($1k to $5k and up).** The profit center. Often application-based, which means the call to action fits neatly in a P.S. Optional, but potent.
3. **Recurring Income.** For most businesses, the most important spoke. A monthly membership or subscription, often in the $30 to $50 range, which is a sweet spot. High enough to attract real buyers, low enough that they don't re-justify it every month. And here's the math that matters: say you sell a course for $100. If your average subscriber stays on a $20-per-month membership for ten months, you've doubled that revenue. Some businesses build the entire company around ONE recurring offer and point nearly every email at it. A single clear North Star offer is a powerful thing.
4. **Promos and Stories.** Promos are cash injections. Run one to two a month max, each with real urgency, a hard deadline, and a reason why it exists AND why it ends. Plus roughly one pure story email a week, and occasionally a story with NO link at all, because that builds buyers for years.

The formula: Low-Ticket plus High-Ticket plus Recurring plus Promos equals a sustainable business. But don't overcomplicate it. Plenty of people make millions on a single core offer. Build the wheel only as far as you need it.

### One email, one idea

One email equals ONE tip, one idea, one outcome. Don't cram.

There are three archetypes of email writer:

- **The Used Car Salesman.** Always pitching. Churn-and-burn. Dead list.
- **The Teacher.** Gives everything away, trains the list to never buy (and every list gets trained by its owner, whether you mean to or not).
- **The Speaker.** Entertains, educates, and always leaves them wanting more.

Be the Speaker. And kill the fear of selling. It's the number one thing holding people back. Selling a good product IS helping. If you believe in what you sell, withholding it isn't humility, it's a disservice.

### Subject lines

The subject line is the email's headline, and the headline is everything. It's where you get their attention. Picture your own inbox: all the junk, the spam, the stuff you don't care about. Your subscribers' inboxes look the same, and you are not special. So ask yourself what you can send that actually gets their attention.

Helpful works. Provocative works. Controversial works extremely well. And here's the biggest tip: if you're emailing about an offer, you already built the page, which means you already wrote the headline. Just use variations of that headline as your subject line. Your AI can spin those variations for you if it has the right context.

Dan Kennedy said if you had a set amount of time to write a sales letter, spend 90% of it on the headline and the first paragraph. The same rule applies to email. The subject line sets the tone and the expectation. Its one job is to get the open, and from there the subject line's job was already done. If they don't open, nothing else you wrote matters.

The formula that almost always wins: Curiosity plus Benefit. Tease something they want to know AND something they want to get. Backup formulas: How-To direct benefit, Shock and Awe, Personal Story, Question.

One non-negotiable: never clickbait something the body can't deliver. The subject line is a promise. Break it and you train the list to distrust you, which is the exact opposite of what you're building.

Long-term, the goal is to make your subject lines almost irrelevant because your FROM name does the work. People open because it's you. Until you're there, the subject line matters enormously.

The big finding from large-scale testing: subject lines move clicks and SALES, not just opens. In head-to-head tests with serious volume, the winning subject line beat the loser by hundreds of percent on downstream results, not just open rate. Two emails with identical bodies and different subject lines can produce wildly different sales. The subject line is not a cosmetic, it's a lever.

### The email-type menu

This is an idea-generation menu: ten reliable email structures. When you don't know what to write, pull one off the rack.

1. **Mr. Miyagi (soft teaching).** Give the WHAT for free, charge for the HOW. Being SHOWN how to actually do it is what's worth paying for. Hard teaching gives everything away and leaves nothing to want. Soft teaching leaves the link. Structure: hook, story, lesson, how it applies to their goal, call to action, casual P.S. Front-load a real takeaway, show proof, hold back the full implementation.
2. **Correlations.** Bridge two unrelated things (fly fishing and email, a road trip and a sales lesson). Confusion becomes curiosity becomes "aha," and people value what they feel they discovered themselves. The borrowed thing's emotions transfer onto your offer. Kill jarring pivots with one smoothing sentence that bridges the gap. Variant: tie it to something newsworthy that's already in their head. You get free read-through by riding existing attention.
3. **RVL (Results, Value, Lifestyle).** A three-option menu. Results: case studies, before-and-afters, including your own. Value: put real value in the email, link in the P.S. Lifestyle: sell the thing under the thing. Not the course, but what the course lets you do with your time.
4. **Stories.** The one word is TENSION. Build it, hold it, release it at the last possible moment. Revealing the outcome too early kills the story. The whole engine is the unresolved question. Hero origin structure: had it bad, vivid pain, the breakthrough, it worked for me, I systematized it, now it works for you, here's the shortcut. Everyday stories work too: stubbed toe, lesson, product. Capture every mildly interesting moment in a phone note. That's your story bank. Stories must be TRUE. Dramatize the moment, never the facts. The scariest story to tell usually connects the hardest.
5. **Best Way Without.** Sell the result WITHOUT the hated grind. A six-pack without giving up pizza and beer. Find what your audience hates about the conventional path (the time, the embarrassment, the cost, the boredom) and remove it. Repositioning alone can lift response meaningfully.
6. **Worst Way.** Loss aversion. People fight harder to avoid losing $100 than to gain $1,000. Expose a real flaw in the conventional approach, make that disaster feel preventable, then open the escape hatch: your solution.
7. **Paradigm Shift.** Challenge a foundational belief and the reader has to keep going to resolve the dissonance. Three requirements or it's just contrarian noise: it must be genuinely insightful, defensible with evidence, and more useful than the old belief. Your product is the gateway to the new reality.
8. **80/20.** Two flavors. The 80/20 Path teaches the principle and applies it to their problem. The 80/20 Correlation ties it to something concrete. Both show WHAT the big wins are and gate the HOW.
9. **The FAQ email.** Write one for every promo. Harvest real questions first (a one-line "Questions?" email or a P.S. asking). Then bold the question, give a plain answer, and put a sales link after every answer. Five questions, five links, because people skip the questions they don't have and click the one they do. Send it on the LAST day of the sale, close to the deadline. It's one of the highest-converting emails in any promo.
10. **Calls to Action.** The email's job is to set up the click. Six CTA types:
    - "Click here." A plain embedded command. The workhorse. Boring works.
    - Link text that matches the subject line. Congruent, triggers recognition, great for content links.
    - Reply CTA. Ask them to reply. Demand-tests a product before you build it, and replies are deliverability gold.
    - P.S. CTA. A soft pitch that can ride on any email regardless of topic.
    - Domain-signature sign-off. A hyperlinked domain under your name. Few clicks, but hot ones.
    - "Ways to work with me" P.S. List your offers priced high to low to anchor the reader against the top number.
    - Placement: put the main link toward the bottom. Early links get clicks, but often the wrong clicks from people who didn't read. One link, one focus. Roughly 90% of your emails should link somewhere. You're not running a charity newsletter.

### The 5 essential automations

If you only build a few things, build these. Write once, sell forever.

1. **Welcome sequence (5 to 7 emails, daily, sell early).** Buy likelihood peaks right after opt-in, while interest is hottest. If your prospect has a painful problem, they don't want it solved five days from now. Making them wait doesn't make you an altruist, it makes you the opposite. Sell early. After the sequence, drop them into your regular broadcast pool.
2. **Nurture or ascension sequence (optional).** A longer runway before broadcasts for businesses with a longer trust cycle.
3. **Abandoned action (3 to 5 emails).** Not just abandoned carts, ANY unfinished action. A half-done checkout, an incomplete application, a started-but-unsubmitted form. The claim is roughly 30% more sales for almost any company. These are some of the most profitable emails per word that exist, because the person already told you they want it, they just didn't finish.
4. **Re-engagement (trigger around 90 days of no opens).** The centerpiece is a famous nine-word email attributed to Dean Jackson: "Are you still looking for a house in Georgetown?" Swap in your equivalent. Nothing else. The skill here is subtraction: the shorter and more personal, the better. And keep this in mind: in most markets, only a fraction of inquirers buy in the first 90 days, but roughly half buy within 18 months. Most of the money is in the long tail. Never write off the quiet ones too soon. Re-engage before you cut.
5. **Post-purchase consumption sequence (where most businesses fail).** This is the one almost nobody runs, and it's the secret behind the high per-subscriber numbers. It's a sequence whose only job is to get buyers to actually USE what they bought. Course completion rates are famously dismal, and a good consumption sequence can push that dramatically higher. Why it matters for money: customers who succeed with the thing buy again. And the best time to sell someone is right after they've gotten a win from your last product. Consumption drives retention, retention drives lifetime value, lifetime value is the whole game.

Sequence craft across all of them: map the journey, ask for micro-commitments, leave open loops and cliffhangers between emails so they want the next one, ramp the urgency, and make ONE offer at a time. Never two offers fighting in one email.

## Email metrics that matter

- **List growth.** New subscribes minus unsubscribes. You want this positive, but quality over raw size.
- **Delivery rate.** Percentage landing in the inbox vs. sent. Aim for 95% or better.
- **Open rate.** Percentage of delivered emails opened. Keep it healthy (roughly 25 to 35%) for deliverability, but treat it as a softer signal than it used to be given image auto-loading.
- **Click-through rate.** Percentage that clicked. This is closer to truth than opens.
- **Unsubscribe rate.** Percentage opting out per send. Don't obsess, but watch for spikes.
- **Complaint rate.** Percentage marking you as spam. This one you DO watch closely. It directly wrecks deliverability.

The deliverability flywheel: opens up, clicks up, unsubscribes and complaints down, delivery rate up, more of your list actually sees you, more money. Sending the right campaign to people in the right journey stage drives all four levers at once. Sending the wrong thing to everyone breaks all four. Relevance is deliverability.

## How to help someone with this

Don't lecture the framework. Diagnose and build:

1. **Find out how often they email.** If it's less than weekly, that's almost always the first fix before any clever tactic. Frequency is the cheapest lever and the most neglected.
2. **Ask which of the three email types they use.** Most only send promotional blasts. The fix is adding transactional leverage and relational nurture so they earn the right to sell.
3. **Map their list against the five phases.** Which campaigns exist, which are missing? The missing ones (usually indoctrination and post-purchase consumption) are the highest-leverage builds.
4. **Check the automations.** Welcome sequence that sells early? Abandoned action? A consumption sequence? Re-engagement before they cut dead weight? The empty slots are money on the floor.
5. **Audit their offers, not just their copy.** If every email is "buy now," they have an offer-wheel problem, not a writing problem. Get a low-ticket entry point and a clear recurring or core offer in place, then the emails practically write themselves.
6. **Fix the bank account.** If the list is dead, they've almost certainly been all withdrawals. Prescribe a run of pure deposits (stories, value, a laugh, something genuinely useful) before the next ask.

The single most common unlock: send more often, send the right campaign to the right stage, and make the entry offer genuinely great. Almost everything else is detail hanging off those three.
