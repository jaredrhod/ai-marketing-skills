# Lead magnets: how to build the thing that grows your list

**What this is:** the playbook for the offer that turns a stranger into a lead. A lead magnet is something valuable you give away in exchange for someone's contact info. This doc covers what makes one convert, the formats that work, how to research the right topic instead of guessing, and how to check your idea before you build it. Reach for this when you're trying to grow an email list, your opt-in page isn't converting, or you don't know what your free thing should be. It's Step 3 of the CVO funnel, so if you want the whole machine first, hit the CVO doc, then come back here for the tactics.

**Credit where it's due:** the criteria, the format menu, and the benchmarks here come from DigitalMarketer's Customer Value Optimization material (Ryan Deiss and team), where the lead magnet is Step 3 of the funnel. What's mine is what I've watched actually convert running real ad budget to these pages across multiple businesses.

## What a lead magnet actually is

No money changes hands, but value does. The person is consciously trading something they care about (their email, their attention) for something specific you offered. That small commitment is the foundation of every sale that follows. Treat it like a real transaction, because it is one. The currency is permission instead of dollars.

Its only job is to grow your list with people who've told you they care about the problem you solve. Not direct revenue. A lead magnet doesn't pay you back at the opt-in. It pays you back downstream when that list moves through the rest of the funnel: the entry offer, the core offer, the recurring revenue, the return path. So don't judge it on whether it made money right away. Judge it on whether it built a list of the right people.

This is why I run ads to lead magnets at the top of the funnel. The ad's whole job is the opt-in. Some people buy a tripwire offer on the page right after. Most don't, and that's fine, because the sale was never the point. Once they're on the list, they get what they signed up for, then a warm-up sequence of my best stuff, pure goodwill, and now they're mine to sell to for as long as they stay. Even if they never buy off that first page, they have unlimited chances down the road. The lead magnet built the pipeline. The list does the selling.

**Why this is one of the highest-leverage things to optimize:** every percentage point of opt-in conversion multiplies through every downstream step. A page that converts 25% instead of 15% doesn't just give you 67% more leads. It gives you 67% more leads times the entry-offer conversion times the core-offer conversion times the lifetime value of everything after. The improvement compounds through the entire system. Fixing the lead magnet is cheap and it pays everywhere.

## Give away your best stuff

Here's the thing people get wrong. They hold back. They think if they give away the answer, nobody buys. It's the opposite.

Frank Kern taught me this: give away your best stuff, and also sell the stuff you give away. Will some people take everything free and never pay you a dime? Yes. They're real, and they don't matter. Most people don't behave that way. The majority of people want to return the favor when you genuinely help them. Goodwill is a real force. Use it.

One scope note: "give it away and also sell it" works literally when what you sell IS information, like a course or a membership. If you sell a physical product, software, or a service, the free thing is the content and know-how around it, not the product itself. Give away the content, sell the product.

The fear comes from not realizing how much you actually have. Think about the one question everyone always asks you. That's your content. That's your lead magnet. And there's never just one question. There's a whole library in you if you look.

So build something you're actually proud of. Something that helps people for real. That's what makes someone want to stay on your list and eventually buy.

## The 4 elements of a great lead magnet

This is DigitalMarketer's framework (Ryan Deiss and team). All four have to be present. Miss one and conversion drops.

**1. Specificity.** The most important piece, full stop. Your lead magnet has to solve a specific problem with a specific solution for a specific segment of the market. Generic lead magnets ("Free Marketing Guide") underperform every single time. Specific ones ("The 5 Subject Lines Our Best Customers Replied To") punch way above their weight.

The sharper version of this rule: a lead magnet has to answer ONE big question, not a bunch of small ones. If you can't name the single question it answers in one sentence, it's too broad. "Just got a new puppy?" answers one question, and a new puppy owner self-identifies instantly. "Build a solid foundation with any dog, new, rescue, or full-grown" answers three, and it converts worse because nobody reads it and thinks "that's exactly me" fast enough. Don't try to catch every audience with one piece. Different audiences get different lead magnets. The instinct to go broad so you don't "leave anyone out" is the exact instinct that kills conversion.

**2. Instant gratification.** The faster they can consume it and feel the value, the better. A 5-minute checklist beats a 90-minute course. A quiz that spits out a result immediately beats a multi-session program. Don't make people wait to feel like the trade was worth it. The payoff has to land while they still remember opting in.

**3. Unique value.** The prospect should feel "I couldn't get this anywhere else." If the exact same content is sitting free on a blog post or a competitor's site, the perceived value drops to zero. Free but rare beats free but everywhere.

**4. High perceived AND actual value.** Free doesn't mean it should look free. Put real effort into the design, the writing, the structure. If the lead magnet looks slapped together, the prospect assumes the paid product is too. Flip it: if the free thing feels premium, the paid thing feels even more premium. The free thing is an audition for everything you'll ever sell them.

## What's NOT a lead magnet

These get called lead magnets and they're not. They fail one or more of the four elements and they don't convert.

- **"Subscribe to our newsletter."** A newsletter is a delivery mechanism, not a benefit. Nobody opts in for the privilege of receiving more email. If your only offer is "subscribe to my newsletter," you don't have a lead magnet. You have a hope that someone cares.
- **"Free freebie" / "Sign up for free goodies."** No specificity. The reader can't picture what they're getting, so they won't trade an email for it. "Free" is not a benefit. The benefit is the thing.
- **Generic "free guide" with no benefit named.** "Free Marketing Guide." Free guide to what? For what outcome? Vague guides die.
- **"Join our community."** Community isn't a benefit unless the prospect already knows exactly what they'd get from this specific community. Most of the time they don't.
- **"Updates and special offers."** That's sales-speak for spam. Nobody opts in for "updates."

The thread through all of these: they name a delivery mechanism and make the reader figure out the outcome themselves. People don't do that work. You have to name the outcome for them.

## Weak vs strong (the pattern)

| Weak (generic, vague benefit) | Strong (specific, named outcome) |
|---|---|
| "Subscribe to our newsletter" | "Take your blog posts from 'Meh' to magnificent, fast" |
| "Free marketing guide" | "The 5 Subject Lines Our Best Customers Replied To" |
| "Sign up for our updates" | "10 Cold Email Templates That Booked Us 7 Figures In Meetings" |
| "Free freebie" | "Just got a new puppy? Get the 30-day plan to a calm, trained dog" |
| "Join our list" | A free trial of a specific tool (specific access) |
| "Get coupons" | "15% off your first order" |

The strong version names the outcome the reader gets. The weak version names a delivery mechanism and asks the reader to imagine the outcome on their own. They won't.

## Conversion benchmark: what good looks like

Measure the squeeze page (the opt-in page itself, not the asset you're giving away).

- **Below 25%:** there's room to improve. Work the culprits listed below.
- **25 to 35%:** solid. A competent page doing its job.
- **Above 35%:** great. Sometimes that's the best you'll get, and that's totally fine. You can try to push it higher, but anything north of 35 is a real win, not something to apologize for.
- **50%+:** exceptional, and reachable on a narrow, well-targeted offer (specific question, specific segment, the right traffic). My own Confidence Checklist headline clears 50% to cold traffic, and DigitalMarketer's benchmark for a great squeeze page is over half of visitors converting into leads.

The reason elite pages clear 50% is specificity plus the one-big-question rule plus the right traffic. When a visitor sees a page that answers their exact question for their exact situation, the trade is obvious and they barely think about it. When they see a generic offer, they leave.

If your page is below 25%, work the likely culprits in this order:
1. Generic headline that doesn't let the reader self-identify in 2 seconds.
2. Lead magnet promises a category ("marketing") instead of one specific outcome.
3. The page is fighting the offer: too many form fields, too many CTAs, too much noise.
4. Wrong traffic. The visitors aren't actually in the target segment, so even a perfect offer misses.

## The 8 formats that convert

Roughly ordered by how often they get used. Pick the one that fits the audience and the topic, not the one that sounds impressive.

**1. Checklist.** Easiest to build, easiest to consume, often the highest converting because the prospect can scan it in 30 seconds and feel like they got value. Best for tactical how-to topics where the steps are concrete. "10 Things To Audit On Your Sales Page Before You Launch."

**2. Webinar.** Creates urgency (live timing) and uses audio plus visual. Live versions amplify FOMO. Evergreen versions (run through an automated webinar tool) keep working forever. Higher build cost, higher conversion ceiling. Best for high-ticket offers where the thing needs explanation, or topics that need demonstration.

**3. Template.** Hands over outlines or starting points the prospect fills in. "10 Cold Email Templates That Booked Us 7 Figures." Templates are leverage: the prospect feels like they're skipping weeks of work. Best for writing, design, ops, anything with a repeatable shape.

**4. Case study.** Real-life examples. A specific named customer, specific numbers, a specific timeline. They sell trust, not information. Best for B2B, high-ticket, services, anything where social proof drives the decision more than facts do.

**5. Discount.** Direct exchange: email for reduced pricing or free shipping. Best for ecommerce, SaaS, services with a clear price, anything with a recurring purchase decision. One caveat: discounts don't work for every brand. Some sophisticated, premium-positioned audiences read an upfront discount as a desperation signal, and offering one upstream can actively cheapen the brand. If the positioning is premium, a discount may cost you more than it makes.

**6. Software / free trial.** High perceived value when the software solves a real problem. The trial is the lead magnet AND a qualifier in one: only people interested enough to actually try it will sign up, so the leads come pre-warmed. Best for SaaS and anything self-serve.

**7. Quiz / survey.** Highly engaging. The user enters their email to see the results. The bonus, and it's a real one: the quiz segments the list. If you know the prospect's "type," every follow-up email can be tailored to it. That's serious leverage on the back end. Best for anything where the audience has distinct sub-segments worth treating differently.

**8. Sample.** A physical or digital sample of the actual product. First chapter free. First module free. Works because of instant gratification AND because they're tasting the real thing, not a description of it. Best for books, courses, info products, physical goods.

## Picking the format

Don't start with the format. Start with the audience and the problem, then let the answers point you to a format.

1. **What's the highest-volume question your audience is already asking?** If the same question keeps coming up in your DMs, your support inbox, your community, your comments, that question IS your topic. Don't guess. (Full method below.)
2. **How fast does the prospect need to feel value?** Under 5 minutes, lean checklist or quiz. Willing to invest more, webinar or sample.
3. **Do sub-segments need different paths?** If yes, quiz wins (segmentation is leverage). If no, checklist or guide ships faster.
4. **Is the topic visual or demonstrable?** If yes, webinar or video. If no, written formats.
5. **Build cost vs conversion ceiling.** Checklist: about a day, decent conversion. Quiz: about a week, high conversion plus segmentation. Webinar: a few weeks, highest conversion and highest ceiling. Match the investment to the stakes.

## How to research the right topic (the most important section)

The biggest mistake people make: they brainstorm lead magnet ideas in a vacuum. They guess what their audience wants, build it, ship it, conversions disappoint, and they blame the format.

The format isn't the problem. The topic is.

The fix is simple: read what your audience is already asking, in their own words. Don't invent a topic. Discover the one they're already telling you.

### Sources of audience signal (ranked)

1. **Your support inbox.** Pull the last 90 days of incoming questions. Cluster by theme. The biggest cluster is your topic.
2. **Your group / Discord / community.** Pull the last 30-60 days of posts. Filter for questions ("how do I," "why does," "anyone else," "is it normal that"). Cluster them. Highest-volume cluster wins.
3. **Comments on your top-performing content.** If a video, post, or article got disproportionate engagement, the comments tell you what landed. Read them all.
4. **DMs and replies to your emails.** If people reply to your broadcasts, what are they asking for?
5. **Reviews of competitor products,** especially the 1- and 2-star ones. They tell you what the audience expected and didn't get. That gap is the lead magnet.
6. **Reddit, Quora, niche forums.** Search the niche, read the recurring questions.

The whole process: ignore your guesses, read what they're already asking, cluster the questions by theme, build the biggest theme. That's it. The research is reading, not brainstorming.

## Copy and presentation: this is where people leave money on the table

The lead magnet topic and format are half the battle. The copy and the presentation are the other half, and most people get them wrong.

**The headline is everything.** Dan Kennedy said if you had a set amount of time to write a sales letter, spend 90% of it on the headline and the first paragraph. That applies here. Your opt-in page headline has one job: get them to self-identify in 2 seconds and feel like this was made for them. "That's exactly me. I need that." If they have to work to figure out whether it's for them, you've already lost them.

**Plain beats polished.** A shiny, corporate-looking opt-in page almost always performs worse than a simple, plain, non-branded page. The ugly plain-Jane page wins again and again. Put your energy into the words, not the gloss.

**Write like one person talking to another.** No corporate jargon. No "Company X has been in business for X years providing excellent, consistent, high-quality service." Talk to people like you're sitting across the table from them. Use "I," use "you." Write it like a letter to a friend, because that's what converts.

**Sell the benefit, not the product.** You're always selling the outcome, never the thing itself. To drill down to the real benefit, ask "so what?" and keep asking until you can't anymore. "Get my free horse training checklist." So what? It teaches you to solve your horse problems. So what? Then you actually enjoy the time you spend with your horse. So what? Then you enjoy your life more. Once you can't ask "so what?" again, you've hit the real benefit. Sell that in your headline and your opt-in copy.

## Diagnostic checklist (run this before building anything)

1. **Can you name the ONE big question this answers in a single sentence?** If you catch yourself saying "and also," or "for X people, Y people, AND Z people," the scope is too wide. Pick one.
2. Does it solve a specific problem (not "marketing," but "subject lines that don't get filtered to spam")?
3. Can the prospect get value within 5 minutes of opting in?
4. Is the value something they couldn't easily get elsewhere?
5. Does the design and writing match the quality of your paid product?
6. Did you research the topic from actual audience signals, not guesses?
7. Is there a clear next step after they consume it (the offer, the email sequence, the tripwire)?
8. Does the title promise the benefit, not just tease curiosity? Tell them what they get, don't make them wonder.
9. Does the opening line let the reader self-identify in 2 seconds? ("Just got a [thing]?" / "Struggling with [specific problem]?") If they have to work to figure out whether it's for them, rewrite it.

If any answer is no, fix it before launching. Every "no" is a leak in a page that multiplies through your whole funnel.

## The one thing to remember

Specificity wins. One big question, one specific segment, one named outcome. The temptation is always to go broad so you don't leave anyone out, and going broad is exactly what makes the page convert worse. Narrow it until one person reads the headline and thinks "that's me." That person opts in. The "everyone" you were trying to catch never does.

And give away your best stuff. The list you build with something genuinely valuable is worth ten times the list you build with filler. The goodwill pays back, and the people who were never going to buy don't matter anyway.
