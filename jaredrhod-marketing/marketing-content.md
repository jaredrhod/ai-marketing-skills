# Content marketing: the library that moves people through the funnel

**What this is:** the playbook for the content side of marketing, the actual assets you build (blog posts, videos, podcasts, social posts, lead magnets, sales pages) and where each one sits in the funnel. The CVO funnel is the machine; content is what you feed into it at every stage. When someone says "I'm posting a ton of content and nothing converts," or "what should I even make," or "I get traffic but no leads," start here. This assumes you already know the CVO funnel (traffic to lead magnet to tripwire to core offer to profit maximizer). If not, go there first.

**Credit where it's due:** the funnel-stage model and the campaign-planning structure in here come from DigitalMarketer's Ultimate Guide to Digital Marketing, Chapter 2. What I'm adding is what I've seen actually move money when you build these libraries for real businesses.

The first thing to get straight: content marketing is not blogging. Blogging is one channel. Content marketing is the entire library of assets you build to move prospects through every stage of the funnel. People hear "content" and picture a blog. They grind out posts for a year, get traffic, and make no sales, then they decide content doesn't work. Content works fine. They built one third of the library and skipped the two thirds that actually convert.

## Every piece of content lives at one of three funnel stages

TOFU, MOFU, BOFU. Top, middle, bottom. Build a full library across all three or you're leaving money on the table. This is the most important idea in the whole doc: the stage a piece of content sits at determines its job, and most businesses only build one stage.

### Top of funnel (TOFU): awareness

Job: make cold prospects aware their problem exists AND that a solution exists. Low barrier, no email required, no money. The content is just out there, free, for anyone.

What lives here: blog posts, social media updates, infographics, photographs, digital books, audio podcasts, video and video podcasts, microsites, print newsletters, primary research.

The bar every TOFU piece has to clear: it must entertain, educate, or inspire. If it does none of those, it doesn't earn the click and it doesn't earn the share. A cold stranger owes you nothing. The content has to be worth their attention on its own merits before you've earned the right to ask for anything.

And here's the thing about giving away good TOFU content: give away your best stuff. I know that feels backwards. It's not. Frank Kern taught me this, and he was right. Giving away genuinely good content creates goodwill and people want to return the favor. Will some people take everything for free and never pay you a dime? Yes. They don't matter. Most people don't behave that way, and the ones who were going to buy are now far more likely to because you actually helped them first. The fear of giving too much comes from not realizing how much you have. If you can only think of one tip to give away, you don't know your niche well enough yet.

### Middle of funnel (MOFU): evaluation and lead generation

Job: convert "problem aware" and "solution aware" prospects into leads. The prospect trades their contact info for a higher-value piece of content. That higher-value piece is the lead magnet.

What lives here: educational resources (case studies, white papers), useful resources (swipe files, checklists), software downloads, discount or coupon clubs, quizzes and surveys, webinars and events.

This is where conversion happens. Read that again. Most businesses skip MOFU entirely, then wonder why all their TOFU content doesn't convert. It doesn't convert because there's nothing for it to convert into. TOFU creates awareness; MOFU captures it. No MOFU, no leads, and the traffic evaporates.

A note on building the list here: a list of emails or phone numbers is something you actually own. Build a following on YouTube, TikTok, Instagram, anywhere you can. But understand the catch: you don't own that audience. One account flag and it's gone overnight. So build the following, but always be converting it into a list you control. MOFU is where that happens.

### Bottom of funnel (BOFU): conversion

Job: convert leads into customers. By now the prospect is basically ready to buy. They're comparison-shopping. They need the final push and the last bit of proof.

What lives here: demos and free trials, customer stories, comparison sheets ("X vs Y"), webinars and events, mini-classes.

The customer-story format is worth dwelling on because most people do it wrong. "Our customers love us" is not a customer story. A customer story is a named person, a specific result, and a specific timeline. All three. "Sarah, a bakery owner, cut her food waste 30 percent in 60 days using our inventory tool" is a customer story. The specificity is what makes it believable, and believable is what closes. Vague testimonials are decoration. Specific ones are proof.

## The 6 characteristics of content that actually works

Run every piece you're about to build through this list. If it fails one, fix it before you publish.

### 1. Full funnel

Don't stop at TOFU. Build content for MOFU and BOFU too. This is the most common mistake in the whole game: people publish blog posts forever and wonder why the traffic doesn't turn into money.

Here's the move that fixes it, and it's counter-intuitive: build MOFU and BOFU first. Plan the whole funnel backwards, start from the last thing that happens to a customer and work back, then build the lead magnets and the customer stories before you go chasing real TOFU traffic. Most people do the opposite: they spend a year building an audience with no way to capture or convert it, then bolt on a lead magnet as an afterthought. Flip it. Build the catch-net first, then go make it rain. One clarification, because it trips people up: you can absolutely start making top-of-funnel content and dropping your pixel before the capture exists. That content is just building awareness, it isn't sending anyone to an opt-in yet, so nothing is wasted. The mistake isn't making content early. It's building a whole library of it with nothing for the traffic to convert into. Make content and grow your pixel from day one. Just have the catch-net built before you start driving people to opt in.

### 2. Intent based

Content has to satisfy what the prospect is searching for at that moment. A pricing page is content. A comparison sheet is content. The most lucrative content meets existing intent, because someone Googling "FreshBooks vs QuickBooks" has already decided to buy one of them, they're just picking. Content that meets that person closes way harder than content that has to first convince a stranger they have a problem.

Practical move you can do in 30 seconds: use Google autocomplete. Type your competitor's name, or your category, into the search bar and watch what it suggests. Those suggestions are real searches real people are actually typing. "Xero vs QuickBooks" showing up in autocomplete is a content asset sitting there waiting for you to build it. The search engine is literally telling you what people want.

### 3. Ascension focused

Every single piece of content needs a next step. A blog post needs an embedded ascension offer: a lead magnet, a related post, a demo, something. A pricing page needs a free-trial CTA. If you publish content with no path forward, you built a dead end, and a dead end is wasted attention. You got someone's eyeballs and then said nothing. Never let a piece of content just stop.

### 4. Segmented

When a prospect engages with a piece of content, they've raised their hand on a topic. That's a signal, and signals are gold. Use it. Tag them by what they engaged with, retarget them, send them offers tailored to what they just showed interest in. Someone who read your post on a specific problem is a different person than someone who read a different post, and they should get different follow-up. Don't treat your whole list like one blob.

### 5. Cross channel

One content asset gets republished across every channel you've got: your website, then the social platforms, YouTube, your podcast feed. You did the work to make the thing once. Squeeze every drop of exposure out of it. Don't build a new asset for every channel; take one good asset and spread it everywhere.

### 6. Avatar based

Each piece of content targets a specific customer avatar. Different avatars need different content, even when the underlying product is identical. The same offer sold to two different types of buyer needs two different pieces of content, because the two buyers care about different things, fear different things, and search for different things. A webinar built for the technically-curious buyer and one built for the total-beginner buyer can sell the exact same product and share almost no copy. Know which avatar each asset is for before you build it.

## The content campaign plan

Plan content at the offer level before you build it. Five columns. The discipline of filling in all five forces you to answer the questions that separate a piece of content that works from one that just exists:

- **Marketing funnel:** is this TOFU, MOFU, or BOFU? If you can't answer, you don't know the asset's job.
- **Avatar(s):** which customer avatar does this target?
- **Vehicle:** text, image, video, or audio?
- **Channel:** where will this get published?
- **Ascension path:** what's the call to action? What's the next step?

That last column is the one people leave blank, and a blank ascension column means you're about to build a dead end. Fill it in before you build a single thing.

## The metrics that actually tell you if it's working

Don't measure content by vanity numbers. Measure it by movement through the funnel:

- **Traffic by channel:** where is awareness coming from? This is your TOFU health check.
- **Net new MQLs (Marketing Qualified Leads):** middle-of-funnel conversion. Are you turning traffic into leads?
- **Conversion rate:** page visits divided by conversions on your lead forms, product pages, and CTAs.
- **Net new SQLs (Sales Qualified Leads):** bottom-of-funnel signals like demos requested and customer-story views. Are leads turning into buyers-in-waiting?

These map directly to the three funnel stages. Traffic measures TOFU, MQLs measure MOFU, SQLs measure BOFU. If one number is healthy and the next one downstream is dead, that's where your library has a hole. Go fill the hole.

## How to diagnose someone else's content problem

Don't lecture the framework. Diagnose their library:

1. Ask what content they're making. Nine times out of ten it's all TOFU with no MOFU and no BOFU. That's the diagnosis right there: they built the awareness stage and skipped the two stages that convert.
2. Tell them to build MOFU and BOFU first. A lead magnet so traffic has somewhere to convert, and a few real customer stories (named person, specific result, specific timeline). This is the fastest path to revenue, and it's the opposite of what they want to do.
3. Find the dead ends. Pull up their existing content and ask what the next step is on each piece. The ones with no ascension path are leaking attention. Add a CTA to every one of them.
4. Run their next few ideas through the five-column plan out loud (funnel stage, avatar, vehicle, channel, ascension path). The blanks they can't fill in are the weak spots.
5. For intent, have them open Google autocomplete on their category and their competitors right then. There's almost always a comparison or "how to solve X" asset sitting in the suggestions that they can build this week and that meets buyers at the exact moment of intent.

The headline they need to hear: posting more TOFU won't fix a conversion problem. Building the missing middle and bottom of the library will.

## One more thing: if you can't think of what to create, that's a knowledge problem

If you sit down to build content and you don't know what to make, that means you don't know enough about your niche yet. Become the expert. The more you know your customers' actual problems, the more you realize you have an endless supply of things to say. Think about the questions people always ask you. There's never just one. That's your content. That's your lead magnet. That's your email sequence. It's already there, you just haven't pulled it out yet.

And when you sit down to build any of this, feed your AI the same depth you'd want a real person to have. Give it your customer avatar, the questions your customers actually ask, and your own knowledge base. AI slop comes from an AI with no context. AI that sounds like you, speaks to your customer's real fears, and sells the feeling instead of the product? That comes from an AI with the right context loaded first.
