# Copywriting: the practical playbook

**What this is:** the direct-response copywriting deep-dive. This is what gets SAID once the funnel architecture is in place. The machine is the funnel, the words are what move people through it: headlines, leads, body copy, offers, emails. If someone asks how to write a headline, a subject line, a sales page, or an email sequence, or asks "why isn't my copy converting," start here. If they're asking how the whole funnel fits together structurally, answer that first, then come back here for the words.

**Where this comes from:** none of the underlying frameworks are mine. This is the direct-response lineage: David Ogilvy, Gary Halbert, Eugene Schwartz, Claude Hopkins, John Caples, Dan Kennedy, Frank Kern, Ryan Deiss and DigitalMarketer, Bob Bly, and the AWAI swipe-file tradition. I learned from their books, their videos, and from watching people who are genuinely great at this work. The frameworks below are the ones they proved, in the order you actually use them, with the strong opinions kept in.

> People don't buy products. They buy a better version of their own situation. Write to the transformation, never to the feature.

## The thing that matters most: sell the feeling, not the thing

Before any framework, the most important thing I can tell you about copywriting is this: you're never selling the product.

I sell horse training. But that is not what people are buying. What they actually want is a good relationship with their horse. Nobody buys a training program because they want to train. They buy it because they want to get on and have a good time without fighting their horse the whole way. The training is the path. The feeling is the product.

This applies to whatever you sell. Accounting software? You're selling reassurance: don't go to jail, and don't hand the government a dollar more than they deserve. Fix sinks? You're selling the comfort of knowing your house won't flood. Roses? Most buyers are men buying for their wife. You're selling brownie points. Find the feeling first. Sell that.

So your headline calls out what they get and how they'll feel, not what the thing IS. Every line of copy is about the customer. Never about you, never about the product.

And here's my gut check on whether a page is any good: does it make me think? If I read it and nothing gets stirred up in my head, no second thought, no follow-up question, it's weak. The fastest way to write bad copy is to describe the product or name a feature. "This thing does this." Nobody cares. How does that feature change their life? How does it get them closer to what they want? That's the only question that matters.

## The "So What?" test: how you actually find the outcome

Ryan Deiss taught me this one and it's the most useful drill I know. For every claim, feature, or statement in your copy, ask "So what?" Keep asking until you hit the thing the reader actually cares about emotionally. That's the feature-to-benefit ladder, and you do not stop at the feature. You climb.

The template: **"[The thing the product does], so you can [how that actually helps you]."**

Sophisticated readers (which is most people with money to spend) will not do the work of climbing the ladder for you. They skim past. You have to hand them the payoff already chewed.

### Worked example

- **First-pass claim (feature-level):** "It remembers everything you told it last time."
- So what? You don't have to re-explain your whole situation every time you come back.
- So what? You skip the setup and go straight to your actual question.
- So what? You get the answer faster, without retyping background you already shared.
- **Payoff:** You actually keep using the thing instead of quitting because it makes you do work just to ask a question.

The first-pass line stops at the FEATURE. The "so what?" chain lands the BENEFIT. The feature becomes the proof underneath the headline, not the headline itself.

### Worked example (credibility version)

- **First-pass claim:** "Built from decades of real-world expertise."
- So what? You're not getting generic internet mush that sounds right but isn't.
- So what? You're getting answers from a source that actually knows the domain.
- **Payoff:** You can act on the advice without worrying it'll steer you wrong.

### How to apply it

When drafting any headline, subhead, bullet, subject line, or section opener:

1. Write the first-pass claim.
2. Ask "so what?" three to five times until you hit the emotional payoff.
3. Rewrite with the payoff as the surface line. The feature drops down underneath as the supporting evidence.
4. If you can't get a clean payoff out of a claim after a few rounds, the claim probably isn't worth keeping at all.

Run it on every claim before you ship.

## Prescriptive beats diagnostic, every time

Diagnostic copy points at the problem. Prescriptive copy points at the fix. The fix always wins.

| Diagnostic (weak) | Prescriptive (strong) |
|---|---|
| Why your customers churn | How to cut churn by 40 percent in 90 days |
| What's wrong with your funnel | How to fix the leaks in your funnel |
| The reason your ads aren't converting | How to triple your ad conversion rate |

Diagnostic copy makes the reader feel a problem and then leaves them hanging. Prescriptive copy makes them feel the problem AND see the door out. Same length, same effort, completely different conversion.

The rule: promise the outcome. Not describe the problem. Not tease an interesting reveal. The thing they already want.

Run every headline through this:

1. What specifically does the reader GET from clicking? If the honest answer is "they learn something interesting," that's curiosity-only. Rewrite it.
2. Can the reader say "yes, that's what I want" BEFORE clicking? If the best you can get is "hmm, what's that about?", rewrite it.
3. If you're using a Frank Kern parenthetical, does it deliver an action promise? It should read like "and how to [verb] [outcome]" or "without [common pain]." The parenthetical is where you hand the reader the win.

## The headline: the whole game

Dan Kennedy said if you had a set amount of time to write a sales letter, spend 90 percent of it on the headline and the first paragraph. He's right.

The headline's one and only job is to get attention. From there, the headline sells the first sentence. The first sentence sells the second. The second sells the third. All the way down. If the headline doesn't grab them, nothing else you wrote matters, because they never read it.

And this carries everywhere. Your ad copy is a headline. Your email subject line is a headline. A TikTok hook is a headline. A hook and a headline are the exact same thing. Nail the headline and it carries into everything: your Facebook ads, your email subjects, your video openers, all of it.

Plain beats polished, too. Shiny, polished, corporate-looking pages almost always perform worse than a simple, plain, non-branded page. Put your energy into the words, not the gloss.

## The Frank Kern complete formula

> "How [character or method] [achieves the desirable thing] (and how YOU can do the same)"

The parenthetical is NOT optional. The first half names a character and an outcome, which gives the reader proof and a story. The parenthetical hands that same outcome directly to the reader, which is the action promise that closes the click. Both halves required.

Example:

> *How a former schoolteacher built a six-figure online business in 18 months (and how you can do the same starting from scratch)*

First half: character plus outcome. Parenthetical: the same outcome handed to the reader, with the obstacle pre-killed. This is the single most powerful headline structure in direct response. It's a Gain headline (see below) with the action promise bolted on.

## The 3 headline strategies (Deiss / DigitalMarketer)

Every clickable headline runs on one of three motivational engines. Pick the engine FIRST, then the words follow. Don't blend all three into one headline, you'll dilute every one of them.

### 1. Social Proof (Piggyback)

Borrow the credibility of someone bigger, more known, or more authoritative than you. The reader already trusts THEM, so when you piggyback on the name or the crowd, that trust transfers to you.

People read reviews before they buy. Most people trust online reviews almost as much as a personal recommendation. They want to see that other people (especially people like them) like the thing before they risk their own money. Social proof headlines tap directly into that.

Examples:
- "Why Writers Are Obsessing Over This Grammar App"
- "Drop Shippers Doing $10k+ a Month Are Switching to This Automation Tool (Here's Why)"
- "5,000 Agencies Used This App to Hire Freelance Designers"

**Supercharged version:** piggyback on a high-authority name, not just a crowd. "What [Famous Chef] Says About This Knife Set" beats "What 1,000 Customers Say About This Knife Set," because authority compounds on top of social proof. A big trusted name does more work than a big anonymous number.

### 2. Threat

Show pain to AVOID. Not literal threats, just a pain the reader can dodge by clicking. The headline answers: what kind of pain are we talking about, and how do I sidestep it?

Examples:
- "13 Things to Do So You Don't Lose Your Dog"
- "Read This Checklist Before You Fly to a Foreign Country"
- "The 5 Biggest Mistakes New CrossFitters Make"
- "Are You Overpaying for Wine?"

Threat works because loss aversion is wired stronger than gain motivation in most people. Dodging $200 in waste feels more urgent than gaining $200 in profit, even though it's the exact same $200. That asymmetry is why threat headlines punch above their weight.

**The rule that keeps threat clean:** threats name a SYSTEM or a BEHAVIOR, never a human. "The mistakes most beginners make" works. Naming a real competitor or punching at an entire profession gets you in trouble and alienates half the room. Point the threat at a flawed approach or a bad habit the reader can fix, not at a person.

### 3. Gain

The simplest engine, and the default for most headlines. Two-step formula: benefit plus promise. What's the benefit of clicking, and what's the promise you're making?

Examples:
- "10 Last-Minute Gifts You Can Buy Today"
- "15 Dinner Ideas Using Ingredients Already in Your Fridge"
- "10 Easy Workouts for Beginners"

Gain is the default because it's the most direct path: reader gets a benefit, headline names it, click happens. No cleverness required. The Frank Kern complete formula is fundamentally a Gain headline with the parenthetical action promise stapled on.

## The 3 amplifiers (stack these on any strategy)

These multiply any of the three engines above. Stack one or two to sharpen a Social Proof, Threat, or Gain headline.

### Amplifier 1: Add "How To"

Adding "How To" makes the headline instantly tactical. The reader knows they're getting a METHOD, not just a description of a problem.

- "How to Work Out Like a Fitness Model at the Gym" (Social Proof + How To)
- "How to Avoid the Latest Phishing Scam Targeting Direct Deposit" (Threat + How To)
- "How to Create Content That Earns 100+ Links in a Month" (Gain + How To)

"How To" pairs with all three engines. It's the default amplifier for any educational or how-to content.

### Amplifier 2: Communicate time

A specific time frame changes everything. The reader knows exactly what they're trading and what they get back. Vague headlines die against time-bound ones.

- "How to [Desired Result] in [30 Days]"
- "Take 5 Minutes to Get Started with [Blank]"
- "[Quick Quiz] Reveals What You Should Cook Tonight"

Time can specify a duration ("in 30 days"), a short window ("in 5 minutes"), or a deadline ("by tomorrow"). Always reach for a specific number over a vague stretch.

### Amplifier 3: Add fascination

Language that makes the reader stop scrolling and think "wait, that's interesting."

- "These Cows Already Have 5G, and You Don't" (fascination via absurd juxtaposition)
- "What Your Doctor Won't Tell You About [Blank]"
- "The Ugly Truth About [Blank]"

Fascination runs on incongruity, secrecy, contradiction, or a curiosity gap. Use it sparingly, because overused it reads as clickbait and kills trust. Best when the content actually delivers on the intrigue.

## How to actually draft a headline (the workflow)

1. **Pick the strategy** based on audience and offer. Borrowing authority (Social Proof)? Pointing at pain to dodge (Threat)? Promising a benefit (Gain)? Pick ONE.
2. **Pick one or two amplifiers** to sharpen it. "How To" for tactical content, Time for urgency, Fascination for the curiosity gap.
3. **Run it through the prescriptive test.** Does it promise the benefit? Can the reader say "yes, that's what I want" in two seconds?
4. **Reach for the swipe formulas below** when you're stuck. Fill in the blanks with your audience's ACTUAL words, not the words you'd use about them. Then test two or three versions.

## The Big Idea naming pattern

Every campaign gets ONE branded concept that becomes the handle people grab onto. The Big Idea names the thing.

Examples that worked at scale:

- **"The Atkins Diet."** "Eat fewer carbs" has no handle. "The Atkins Diet" is a thing you can put in your head and tell a friend about.
- Naming a METHOD (e.g. "The [Branded] System") turns a process into a product.
- Naming a CONCEPT or an OUTCOME (e.g. a coined two-word phrase) gives an abstract benefit a shape.
- Sometimes a coined name even beats the real technical term, because the unfamiliar name creates intrigue the plain word can't.

Why naming matters: a name turns an abstract benefit into a THING the prospect can hold. "Stop overthinking it" has no handle. A named system does. When you build a Big Idea, the name should be ownable and memorable, and it should name either the MECHANISM ("The [X] System") or the OUTCOME (a coined phrase for the result). One per campaign, and it shows up everywhere.

## Lead types (the first 100 words)

Pick ONE lead and run it clean. Don't blend.

### Story lead

Open with a specific scene or a specific person. Specific details equal believability. Vague openings read as fiction.

- The classic example: an everyday person stumbles onto something (a recipe, a method, a discovery), and the story carries the reader straight into the pitch.
- The famous Wall Street Journal control: "Two young men" leave the same college and take different paths. That letter ran for decades.

When to use: emotional or trust-building campaigns. It's the default for high-ticket, long-form copy.

### News lead

Tie to a current event the prospect already knows about.

- "Yesterday [Company] launched [Product]..."
- The risk: it only works if your audience is actually following that news. If they're not, the hook lands on nobody.

### Prediction lead

Attach a specific date to a future outcome. The specificity creates intrigue.

- "[Specific Month, Year]: The Beginning of the End."
- Works best in finance, scarcity domains, anything high-stakes where a dated prediction feels urgent.

### Problem lead (diagnostic, then reframe)

Open with the prospect's pain, then immediately reframe it into your solution.

- "If your funnel converts at 1 percent, the problem isn't your traffic. It's your headline."
- Works when the reframe IS the product. Critical: don't camp on the problem. Name it, then pivot fast. Sitting in the pain too long just depresses the reader.

### Big promise lead

Open with the outcome itself.

- "How to make your customers buy three times more often."
- Usually used as the headline. Rarely as the opening line of the body.

## Write like a friend, not a company

Nobody wants corporate jargon. "Company X has been in business for X years providing excellent, consistent, high-quality service." Nobody gives a damn about that.

Talk to people like you're sitting across the table from them. Every sales page I write is first person and opens like a letter. "Hey" or "Dear so-and-so," like I'm talking to a friend, not a prospect I'm trying to close. If more big companies wrote this way, they'd make a lot more money. They won't, which is why you'll beat them.

Long sales pages work too, especially on older audiences. Less than 1 percent of people read one all the way through. Everybody skims. So the whole trick to long-form is your bolding and italics: make the important parts stand out so hard that someone can skim only the highlights and still understand the entire offer and decide to buy. The people who DO need every detail before they'll buy have it all right there. You serve the skimmer and the digger on the same page.

## Body structure patterns

### Reframe, Reveal, Rebuild

1. Take a thing the prospect believes is true.
2. Show them why they're wrong, with proof.
3. Replace it with a better mental model.
4. Tie that new model to your offer.

Universal pattern. Works in any niche where a common assumption is actually wrong, which is most niches.

### David vs Goliath

Works at full strength in adversarial niches: finance (Wall Street vs the average person), health (Big Pharma vs natural approaches), education (gatekeepers vs the self-taught). You set up a villain, cast the prospect as David, and make your product the slingshot.

This structure drives enormous click-through rates when done right, because a shared enemy bonds the reader to you fast.

**Same rule as Threat headlines:** never target a real named person, never punch outward at an entire profession or a political bloc. The villain is a SYSTEM or a BEHAVIOR, not a human. "The system is rigged against you" works. "[Named person] is screwing you" gets you in trouble and alienates half the room.

### Dimensionalize the stakes

Don't say "things will get bad." Paint multiple specific scenes. Stack three to five.

The classic build in finance copy: empty retirement checks, workers losing pay, shortages, unrest, lockdown. Each scene specific and sensory.

Three scenes is good. Five is better. Every scene should be something the reader can SEE, not an abstraction. "It'll be bad" is a yawn. A specific picture is a gut punch.

### Secret reveal

Tease the mechanism, build anticipation, then deliver. **Make the reveal simple.**

- Tease a "magic investment," then reveal it's a calendar-based pattern. Simple.
- Tease a Wall Street dirty secret, then reveal "computers make 70 percent of trades." Simple.

If the reveal is complicated, the promo dies. Pre-tease big, deliver clean. The reader has to be able to repeat the secret to themselves in one sentence, or the anticipation you built collapses into confusion.

### Pretext benefit setup

Make the prospect imagine the SOCIAL win, not just the personal one.

- "You'll feel like a genius when you..."
- "Your friends will ask what you've been doing differently."
- "People will wonder how you suddenly know all this."

The picture of OTHER people noticing is often a stronger close than the benefit itself. Status is a deeper motivator than most people admit.

### Transubstantiation (the Glazer-Kennedy technique)

Take something universally familiar and trusted, then connect it to your unfamiliar product. You borrow the credibility of the known thing.

The textbook example: the chocolate chip cookie recipe. Most popular cookie in the country, around for generations, billions sold. Connect that ("a proven recipe that works year after year") to an info product or a method, and the reader's acceptance of the cookie recipe transfers to your offer. They already believe the cookie recipe is real and durable. The connection lends that belief to you.

Look for universally known stories, universally trusted institutions, universally accepted truths. Tie your offer to one.

### Recruitment frame (frame flip, reciprocity, exclusivity)

The strongest structure for reactivating dormant customers, winning back lapsed subscribers, premium upgrades, and "we want a specific kind of buyer" campaigns. Three layers stacked:

1. **Frame flip (buyer becomes participant).** The pitch is not "buy this," it's "we need you for something." The prospect is being RECRUITED, not sold to. Their sales-defense radar doesn't fire, because the structure isn't a pitch. Examples: "We're looking for testers." "We need 50 case studies." "We're inviting former members into a charter program."

2. **Reciprocity (a gift that feels earned).** Pair the recruitment with real value, but frame it as the thank-you for participating, not as a discount on the product. "We don't normally do this, but since we need your help, here's [X] just for you." The gift feels earned because they're doing YOU a favor.

3. **Exclusivity (a status signal).** Only this audience gets this offer. "First 100 members." "Former subscribers only." "By invitation." The prospect feels CHOSEN, not pitched.

Stack all three and the prospect opens the email feeling chosen, reads the offer feeling like they're getting something rare, and the price tag at the end reads as a thank-you instead of a sales pitch.

Where it fits: warm-but-dormant audiences (former customers, lapsed subscribers), premium upgrades to existing customers, beta or tester programs for new products. Classic proven controls in this style were membership and subscription letters built on "we're looking for a few people like you" framing.

Where it does NOT fit: cold acquisition (the recipient doesn't know you, so the recruitment frame has nothing to stand on), and commodity products (no reason for a recruitment frame around a box of nails).

## Proof and credibility stack

Three layers. Use all three in any long-form piece.

1. **Authority.** Borrowed credibility from named institutions and experts. A named, credentialed source beats "studies show" every time. "A 38-year study out of [Named University]" beats "research suggests." Borrow from sources that are named, credentialed, and recognizable.

2. **Specificity.** Exact numbers, dates, places, names. "23,713 active members" beats "tens of thousands." "On Sunday, May 10th at 7pm Eastern" beats "soon." The specificity IS the proof. Round, vague numbers read as made up. Odd, exact numbers read as real.

3. **Social proof.** Testimonials, case studies, before-and-afters. Letters from real customers beat star ratings every time. Pull SPECIFIC outcomes: "My churn dropped from 8 percent to 3 percent in 60 days" beats "great product, highly recommend."

## Offer construction

### Single focus (the Power of One)

One big idea per piece. One CTA per email. One offer per sales page. Pick the single easiest, most compelling thing to say yes to, and bend all the copy toward it. The moment you offer two things, you've made the reader choose, and a confused reader doesn't buy. They leave.

### Guarantee

I give a long, generous guarantee because I'm confident in the product. Someone who's actually using it isn't going to ask for a refund, because they're getting so much out of it. Think about yourself: you're happy to pay for things that give you real value, and you don't ask for your money back on something that helped you.

The longer and more permissive, the stronger:

- 30 days is the floor.
- 90 days is better.
- 365 days is strongest.
- "Keep the bonus either way" takes the risk to zero.

The thing that scares people off long guarantees is almost never a problem in practice. Most customers won't ask for a refund even when they could. A long, generous guarantee raises conversion meaningfully without raising refund rates much, because it removes the fear that's stopping the sale. "If you don't like it, I'll give you your money back. I'm not going to hold your money hostage, just tell me." That makes saying yes risk-free, and risk-free is what gets people off the fence. You make more money with a generous guarantee, not less.

### Discount mechanics

- Specific dollar amount beats percentage when the number is small. "$12 off" beats "33 percent off" on a $36 product, because $12 feels bigger than the math.
- Percentage beats dollar amount when the number is large. "40 percent off" beats "$60 off" on a $150 product, for the same reason in reverse.
- ALWAYS tie a discount to a reason. "Charter members only," "ends Sunday at midnight," "first 100 signups." A discount with no reason reads as either desperation or a permanent fake-sale. A reason makes it believable.

### Price anchoring

Show the original price, strike it through, then show the lower "for you" price.

- Works for paid programs.
- Doesn't apply to free offers. Nothing to anchor against.
- Heavy-handed anchoring smells like a funnel and erodes trust. Reference the regular price ONCE, then move on. Don't beat the reader over the head with how much they're saving.

## Be transparent about the deal

On every sales page I run a "What's In It For Me?" section, and all it does is tell the truth. Being transparent with your customers is one of the most powerful moves there is.

The reader is already asking the question in their head: "this sounds like a really good deal, I could just buy this and cancel, so why are they doing this?" So answer it. Name the elephant in the room instead of hiding from it. Say it straight: we're giving you this as a bribe to get you to try the subscription. It's a shameless bribe, and it's a good one. If you don't like it, we give your money back and you keep the course free. Then tell them the real reason: you're so confident that once they try it they won't want to leave, and that's exactly what you're betting on.

If there's an elephant in the room, highlight it. Don't pretend it isn't there.

## Email sequences

### Subject lines (the highest-leverage thing in email)

The subject line decides whether anything else you wrote gets read. Treat it exactly like the headline on a sales page, because that's what it is.

If you're emailing about an offer, you already built the page, which means you already wrote the headline. Just use variations of that headline as your subject line.

What works:
- Curiosity plus benefit: "How to make your funnel convert three times more"
- A specific question: "Is [common product] actually a flop?"
- A reframe statement: "Your customers aren't churning"
- Urgency plus benefit: "Tomorrow at 7pm Eastern"
- A specific number: "3 things every founder needs to know about retention"
- Controversial angle: controversial works extremely well. Don't be afraid of it.

What doesn't:
- Pure curiosity with no payoff: "What this expert taught me..."
- Hype caps: "HUGE NEWS!!!"
- Spam triggers: all-caps "FREE," "URGENT," excessive punctuation. They tank deliverability before a human ever sees them.
- Generic teasers: "Quick question for you"
- Fake reply chains: "Re:" or "Fwd:" on a cold email. People resent it the moment they realize, and it burns trust.

Length target: under about 50 characters so it doesn't truncate on mobile.

### Preheader is a second subject line

Mobile clients show roughly 80 characters of preheader text right after the subject. That's free real estate. Use it. Continue the thought, don't waste it on "view in browser."

- Subject: "Your customers aren't churning"
- Preheader: "They're being failed by your onboarding. And that changes everything."

### Multi-email sequences (the 3-email pattern)

Here's roughly what I do, but test your own, because every niche is different. One week I run an offer: open it Wednesday, close it Sunday, send three to four emails in between.

A simple three-email arc that reliably outperforms a single send:

- **Email 1:** Story lead plus big idea plus soft CTA. Open the loop, plant the concept, gentle ask.
- **Email 2:** A different angle (news, a twist, a second story) plus tie back to the big idea plus a stronger CTA. New door into the same room.
- **Email 3:** Last chance. Recap the stories from Emails 1 and 2, make the hardest CTA, and put the deadline in the subject line.

Then a final email a few hours before it closes, sent only to the people who clicked any of those links: "this is literally your last chance." The next week, no pitch at all. Three or four really good content emails, pure goodwill, refilling the account.

The recap in Email 3 matters more than it looks. It builds continuity and rewards the people who opened the earlier emails, and it catches the people who didn't with the whole arc compressed.

### The list is a bank account

Every time you give them content and don't ask for a sale, that's a deposit. Every time you ask for a sale, that's a withdrawal. If everything you ever send is a pitch, you drain the account, and everyone unsubscribes and starts to hate you. So you alternate. Content builds the goodwill. The offer cashes some in.

### Hot-lead immediate send

The moment someone subscribes, registers, or buys, hit them with a follow-up while the excitement is still hot. Same hour, ideally same minute. The "while you wait" or "here's what's next" email sent right after someone raises their hand is one of the highest-converting messages you'll ever send, because you'll never have them warmer than the ten seconds after they said yes. Don't let that heat cool overnight.

### Send timing

We usually send at 9 in the morning or 2 in the afternoon, and that works best for our list. But you'll want to experiment and watch your open rates. The bigger move, if your email tool supports it, is predictive sending (ActiveCampaign does this). Instead of blasting the whole list at once, it sends each person individually at the time they've historically opened your emails. Everybody gets it at their own best moment.

## P.S. discipline

The P.S. is often the second-most-read part of an email, right after the subject line. Skimmers jump to the bottom. Use it.

Do:
- Restate the offer.
- One line of urgency.
- A single link.

Don't:
- Add a new pitch.
- Add a second CTA.
- Use it for unrelated copy.
- Stack multiple P.S. / P.P.S. / P.P.P.S. One P.S. only. The stack of them is an old gimmick that reads as cheap now.

## Long-form sales page architecture

For paid programs and high-ticket pitch pages, layer the page in this order:

1. **Eyebrow plus headline** (Frank Kern formula)
2. **Subhead / deck** (the parenthetical, or the second beat of the promise)
3. **Authority quote** ("[Named source] confirms [the claim]")
4. **Story lead** (a specific scene, a named character)
5. **Reframe** (the big idea, named)
6. **Proof stack** (testimonials, specifics, examples)
7. **Reveal** (the mechanism, kept simple)
8. **Offer** (single, named, with what's inside spelled out)
9. **Guarantee** (long, generous)
10. **Urgency reason** (why now, why this price)
11. **CTA** (one button)
12. **P.S.** (restates the offer plus the link)

## Diagnostic checklist (run every draft through this before shipping)

1. Subject line / headline promises the BENEFIT, not just curiosity.
2. **Every claim passes the "So What?" test.** Drill from feature to emotional payoff. If a claim stops at the feature level, rewrite with the payoff as the surface line.
3. Frank Kern parenthetical is present where appropriate.
4. Lead type chosen and run clean, not blended.
5. Big Idea named, so the piece has a handle.
6. Authority cited (named institutions, experts, sources), not "studies show."
7. Specificity: at least one exact number, date, or name.
8. Title Case for headlines (or your brand's convention). Sentence case for body.
9. **First person from the brand voice** on sales pages, opt-in pages, and emails. No third-person references to the brand anywhere on the page. The reader should feel a person talking to them, not a company describing itself.
10. **Bullets follow benefit-first, reason-after:** [the outcome the reader gets], [the trigger or mechanism that delivers it]. Lead with what they get, then explain why it works.
11. **Every meaningful section stands alone for skimmers.** Most readers skim before they read. Any section they land on cold should make sense and reinforce the offer on its own.
12. **No em-dashes in marketing copy.** Em-dashes are an AI-tell and they quietly kill trust with sophisticated readers. Use commas, periods, parentheses, or colons instead. Hyphens inside legitimate compound words (30-day, well-known) are fine.
13. **Sentence-per-paragraph on long-form sales pages.** Each sentence gets its own line by default. Two very short sentences can share a line. Never 3+ sentences crammed into one paragraph. Dense blocks of text trigger the skim reflex. Sentence-per-line gives the eye anchors and slows the scan into an actual read. Applies to body copy. Testimonials and quotes stay verbatim as the customer wrote them.
14. **Hero CTAs on long sales pages scroll to the OFFER section,** not directly to the order form and not to the top of the letter. Land high-intent clickers at the offer recap plus the "here's what happens when you click" steps plus the actual button. Order-form-direct buttons are reserved for CTAs placed at or after the offer section.
15. Single CTA per email or page (or the two-variant escalation pattern: a short urgent line and a longer descriptive one).
16. P.S. restates the offer plus the link (for emails).
17. **The eye-roll test.** Would the actual reader roll their eyes at this line? If a real person in your audience would smirk and think "yeah, sure," cut it or earn it with proof.

If any item is a no, rewrite before you ship.
